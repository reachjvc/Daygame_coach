## Trunk: Why Reviewing Your Mistakes Makes You Worse

**Target length:** 8-9 minutes

---

### HOOK (0:00-0:50)

A 607-study meta-analysis found something uncomfortable: a third of the time, reviewing your performance makes it worse. Not neutral — actively worse.

The researchers traced why. When review becomes self-critical — when you're judging yourself instead of analyzing the task — learning shuts down. The same process that's supposed to help you improve becomes the thing holding you back.

But here's what's strange. In some cases, review does the opposite. It doesn't just help — it transforms performance. Same person, same activity, different outcome.

So what's the difference?

---

### SEGMENT 1: The Focus Variable (0:50-2:30)

A 2012 rugby study put this to the test. Researchers split players into two groups. Both groups watched video of themselves. Same amount of time, same review process.

The only difference: one group watched their successes. The other watched their mistakes.

Before the next game, they measured testosterone — a marker of physical readiness, confidence, and competitive drive. The players who watched their successes had 28 to 51 percent higher testosterone than the players who watched their mistakes.

Same review process. Different focus. Completely different physiological state.

The mechanism matters here. Self-criticism doesn't just feel bad — it puts the body into a defensive posture. The players watching their mistakes weren't learning from them. They were being worn down by them.

---

### SEGMENT 2: The Convergence (2:30-4:30)

If it was just one study, you could dismiss it. But the pattern keeps showing up.

John Danaher — arguably the most successful grappling coach in history, the guy behind multiple world champions — tells his students not to study the submission. "The lock is just the full stop at the end of a sentence," he says. "It's the sentence that counts."

By the time someone taps, the outcome was already decided. The grip was established. The angle was created. The weight was shifted. The finish was inevitable. The interesting part happened before.

And this isn't unique to BJJ. The US Army's After Action Review format limits discussion to three or four critical moments per operation. Not the whole mission — specific decision points. Surgeons don't review entire procedures. They review the minute before a complication. Poker players don't journal every hand. They journal the hands that felt off. Sports psychologists train athletes to analyze one or two moments per game.

Five completely unrelated fields. Same discovery.

Not because the rest doesn't matter — but because human learning can't process everything at once.

---

### SEGMENT 3: Why Your Brain Already Knows (4:30-6:15)

Here's where the neuroscience connects the dots.

James McGaugh's research on emotional memory found that your amygdala — the part of your brain that processes significance — tags emotionally charged moments for stronger encoding. Moments of surprise, challenge, tension, or high stakes get flagged. They're stored differently. They're remembered more vividly.

A 2022 study by Zheng and colleagues traced the actual pathway: during emotionally charged events, the amygdala signals the hippocampus to consolidate that memory more strongly. It's a built-in importance filter.

This is why the moment you're still thinking about hours later usually is the one worth analyzing. You're not guessing. Your brain already flagged it.

Cognitive Load Theory explains the other side — why trying to analyze everything fails. Working memory holds roughly four to seven items at once. Try to process an entire session — every exchange, every choice, every outcome — and the system gets overwhelmed. The brain protects itself by forgetting most of it.

Exhaustive analysis doesn't fail because you're lazy. It fails because your brain literally can't hold it.

---

### SEGMENT 4: Three Questions (6:15-7:45)

So instead of reconstructing everything that happened, start with which moment you're still thinking about. That's usually the one your brain flagged as significant.

Then three questions:

One. What happened in the thirty seconds before that moment? Not the moment itself — the lead-up. What created the conditions?

Two. What were you trying to do? Not what happened — what was your intention going in?

Three. What would you try differently? Not "what did I do wrong" — what's the adjustment for next time?

One caveat. This assumes you have some experience to build on. Early on, when everything feels foreign, broader documentation helps build pattern recognition. You need volume before you can select.

But that phase ends sooner than most people think. After fifty or a hundred sessions, depth beats breadth. One moment, analyzed well, teaches more than ten moments skimmed.

---

### ENDING (7:45-8:30)

The rugby players who watched their successes weren't ignoring their weaknesses. They were building from a state of readiness instead of defense.

The Danaher students who study the setup aren't skipping the finish. They're analyzing where outcomes actually get decided.

The difference isn't whether you review. It's whether you're feeding your brain the right material.
